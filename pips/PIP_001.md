# Product Improvement Plan (PIP)

## Open Applied AI Atlas

### Goal: Turn the atlas into a practical, trustworthy, easy-to-read knowledge base for decicion guidance on real AI and ML system and application governance, planning, implementation, and operation.

---

## 1. Purpose Of This Plan

This Product Improvement Plan defines how to improve the **Open Applied AI Atlas** as a public knowledge base for applied AI and ML in organizations.

This plan is not a one-shot prompt and not a rigid checklist. It is a live campaign charter for large, phased improvement work. Use it to guide deep repository improvement over many passes.

The atlas already has strong foundations:

* a clear mission
* a broad chapter map
* strong editorial intent
* cross-functional scope
* a durable focus on openness, sovereignty, portability, privacy, compliance, and lock-in

The remaining work is not about changing that direction. It is about making the atlas:

* clearer
* more useful
* easier to read
* more trustworthy
* more practical
* less repetitive
* more willing to rethink its own structure when evidence demands it

---

## 2. Product Objective

Improve the atlas so that it becomes:

* easier for a broad reader to understand
* more direct in how it speaks to the reader
* less abstract and less jargon-heavy
* more useful for real decisions and implementation work
* more evidence-backed where trust matters
* more differentiated by page type and chapter purpose
* more coherent as a whole

The atlas should feel like a strong human guide for real organizational AI work, not like a tidy but partially scaffolded outline or a standards memo written at a distance from the reader.

---

## 3. Non-Negotiable Principles

All changes must preserve and strengthen these principles:

1. **Keep the broad applied AI scope**
   Do not collapse the atlas into LLM-only content.

2. **Keep the organizational view**
   Treat AI systems as organizational systems, not only as models or vendor features.

3. **Keep the cross-functional reader model**
   The atlas must remain useful to business, product, architecture, engineering, ML, QA, security, privacy, governance, sourcing, operations, and leadership roles.

4. **Keep openness, sovereignty, portability, privacy, compliance, and lock-in visible where they matter**

5. **Favor decision utility over completeness theater**
   Pages should help the reader decide, compare, plan, implement, govern, or review.

6. **Favor stronger pages over more pages**
   Symmetry is not a product goal.

7. **Prefer primary sources and explicit evidence where trust-critical claims are made**

8. **Prefer clear, direct language over abstract expert language**

---

## 4. Structural Freedom Doctrine

Nothing in the current atlas structure is sacred.

If evidence or logic shows that the atlas becomes better by changing its structure, then change the structure.

This includes:

* merging chapters or subchapters
* deleting weak or redundant pages
* splitting overloaded chapters
* reordering chapter families
* retitling sections
* renumbering chapters and subchapters
* rescoping chapter boundaries

Structural stability is not a goal by itself. Quality is the goal.

What must stay stable is not the numbering. What must stay stable is:

* mission fit
* cross-role usefulness
* taxonomy coherence
* reader usefulness
* stronger navigation after the change
* less duplication
* more honest evidence posture

If the current chapter map is blocking clarity, then revise the chapter map.

---

## 5. Relevance-First Doctrine

Do not add a section, pattern, artifact, or page just because it exists elsewhere.

Every addition should earn its place by materially improving reader value.

This means:

* do not add `## Evidence Notes` everywhere
* do not add tables where prose is clearer
* do not add diagrams unless they explain something better than text
* do not add worked examples unless they help the reader more than a normal explanation would
* do not add operational artifacts unless they are genuinely reusable
* do not keep a standalone page if it has no strong reason to exist

Restraint is part of quality.

### Evidence Notes Rule

Add `## Evidence Notes` when a page:

* makes a recommendation
* summarizes vendor claims
* summarizes standards or regulatory claims beyond raw text
* mixes direct facts with atlas synthesis or editorial judgment

Use the section to separate:

* primary-source-backed facts
* atlas synthesis
* explicit editorial judgment

Do not add `## Evidence Notes` mechanically.

---

## 6. Writing And Tone Doctrine

The atlas should speak to the reader directly.

It should not drift into distant, abstract, third-person language when direct language would be clearer.

Prefer:

* “Use this page when…”
* “If you are deciding…”
* “Ask…”
* “Check…”
* “Revisit this when…”

Avoid:

* vague institutional language
* detached standards-style prose when a practical sentence would do
* switching between direct address and impersonal third-person voice without reason

Each page should sound like a clear human guide speaking to a reader who is trying to understand a decision, not like a committee note trying to sound formal.

---

## 7. Plain-Language Doctrine

Assume the reader may not know a term, abbreviation, or field-specific phrase.

Write so that a smart reader from another function can still follow the page.

This means:

* prefer common words over specialist jargon when the meaning is the same
* explain abbreviations the first time they appear
* explain technical terms where they matter for the decision
* avoid phrase clusters that sound impressive but hide meaning
* replace abstract expressions with concrete explanations

Bad example:

* “exit posture degradation”

Better examples:

* “it becomes harder for you to switch vendors later”
* “moving this system to another provider gets harder over time”

Bad example:

* “portability posture”

Better example:

* “how easy it is for you to move this system to another stack or provider”

Bad example:

* “shared-control lane”

Better example:

* “use this option when several teams need to share ownership and rules”

The glossary and terminology ledger should support clarity, not excuse unclear writing.

If a page depends on a term, define it in context before leaning on it.

---

## 8. Core Product Problems To Solve

### Problem A: Repetition and boilerplate

Many pages still repeat the same kinds of framing instead of delivering distinct value.

### Problem B: Thin or low-value pages

Some pages still exist more for structure than for reader value.

### Problem C: Not enough decision-grade utility

The atlas still needs more reusable decision support, operational guidance, and worked examples where they matter.

### Problem D: Abstract or insider language

Some pages still rely on jargon, abbreviated thinking, or policy-style language that is harder to understand than it needs to be.

### Problem E: Trust and evidence posture still uneven

Evidence notes, sourcing clarity, and trust-critical distinctions are still too uneven across the atlas.

### Problem F: Structure may no longer match product logic

Some chapter and subchapter boundaries may need to change to support a stronger atlas.

---

## 9. Desired End State

After this campaign, the atlas should show the following qualities:

* chapter front pages are concise and useful
* pages feel distinct and purposeful
* weak pages are merged, rewritten, split, reordered, renamed, or removed
* the chapter map feels earned rather than inherited
* the atlas speaks directly to the reader
* pages are easier to understand without insider vocabulary
* evidence-rich pages are visibly more trustworthy
* major decisions are supported by practical guidance, not only explanation
* the repository feels curated, confident, and readable

---

## 10. Campaign Phases

### Phase 1: Structural Re-Foundation

#### Objective

Improve atlas quality by changing structure where needed.

#### In Scope

* merge weak pages
* split overloaded pages
* reorder chapters
* retitle sections
* renumber freely
* remove low-value pages
* rewrite front doors that no longer match chapter substance

#### Acceptance Criteria

* weak symmetry is reduced
* every page has a strong reason to exist
* chapter boundaries better match actual reader needs
* navigation still works after change

### Phase 2: High-Leverage Chapter Closure

#### Objective

Raise the most important chapter families to a visibly stronger quality level before broad long-tail refinement.

#### Priority Areas

* taxonomy
* use cases and application landscapes
* agentic systems and orchestration
* retrieval and memory
* evaluation, testing, and QA
* security and abuse resistance
* build vs buy and sourcing
* reference architectures
* standards and bodies of knowledge

#### Acceptance Criteria

* the highest-leverage chapters become clearly more useful first
* the atlas improves where readers are most likely to judge its quality

### Phase 3: Evidence And Trust Upgrade

#### Objective

Strengthen trust-critical pages where evidence posture matters most.

#### In Scope

* stronger primary-source coverage
* selective `## Evidence Notes`
* better distinction between fact, synthesis, and judgment
* stronger handling of standards, regulation, vendor claims, and comparison logic

#### Acceptance Criteria

* trust-critical claims are easier to verify
* evidence notes appear where relevant
* evidence posture is clearer and more honest

### Phase 4: Decision-Grade Utility Expansion

#### Objective

Make the atlas more reusable for implementation, sourcing, architecture, and review work.

#### In Scope

* decision guides
* reference architectures
* comparison logic
* worked examples
* operational artifacts
* review templates where they clearly belong

#### Acceptance Criteria

* readers can reuse atlas outputs in real work
* practical utility rises without filling the repo with generic templates

### Phase 5: Readability, Plain Language, And Tone Reconciliation

#### Objective

Make the atlas easier to read and easier to understand across roles.

#### In Scope

* rewrite abstract prose into direct reader-facing language
* reduce jargon where plain words work better
* explain abbreviations when they matter
* normalize tone inside pages
* improve transitions and chapter handoffs

#### Acceptance Criteria

* the atlas sounds more human and less bureaucratic
* a broad reader can understand the pages more easily
* confusing abstractions are reduced

---

## 11. Deliverables

The deliverables should be reader-serving atlas outcomes, not public planning scaffolding.

### Required Outcomes

1. Stronger pages with clearer purpose
2. Better chapter and subchapter boundaries
3. Fewer weak standalone pages
4. Clearer direct-reader language
5. Less unnecessary jargon and fewer unexplained abbreviations
6. Better evidence posture where relevant
7. Better decision aids where justified
8. Better worked examples where justified
9. Better chapter front doors and navigation
10. Updated editorial or contribution guidance only if the improved atlas genuinely needs it

### Not Required By Default

Do not create public meta-docs such as:

* scoreboards
* ledgers
* parity matrices
* public audit inventories
* controller-serving planning artifacts

unless they are truly reader-facing and belong in the atlas.

---

## 12. Success Metrics

### Reader Experience Signals

A new reader should be able to say:

* “I understand what this atlas is for.”
* “This speaks clearly to me.”
* “I do not need insider vocabulary to follow the argument.”
* “This helps me decide, not just absorb terms.”
* “The pages do not all sound the same.”
* “The stronger pages feel trustworthy.”

### Quality Signals

* less repetition
* stronger information density
* stronger chapter differentiation
* clearer direct-reader tone
* plainer language
* fewer weak standalone pages
* stronger evidence posture where needed
* better decision support

### Structural Signals

* chapter reshaping improves the atlas instead of merely moving text around
* renumbering or reordering produces a better long-term structure
* the average page quality rises

---

## 13. Anti-Patterns

Reject the following:

1. preserving weak structure because renumbering feels expensive
2. adding the same section everywhere
3. broad low-signal touching across many chapters
4. abstract, standards-like prose where direct language is possible
5. jargon used instead of explanation
6. unexplained abbreviations
7. keeping pages alive because they already exist
8. treating stability as a higher value than quality
9. filling the repo with public planning scaffolding
10. confusing polish with usefulness

---

## 14. Working Rule

Use this PIP as a live charter for bold, careful, evidence-aware atlas improvement.

Be willing to rethink the structure.

Be willing to delete.

Be willing to rename and renumber.

Be willing to simplify language until a wider reader can understand it.

Be equally willing to stop adding things when they no longer improve the atlas.
