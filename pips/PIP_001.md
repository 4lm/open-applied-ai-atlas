# Product Improvement Plan (PIP)

## Open Applied AI Atlas

### Goal: Turn the atlas into a practical, trustworthy, easy-to-read knowledge base that helps people understand, decide, plan, build, govern, and run real AI and ML systems in organizations.

---

## 1. Purpose Of This Plan

This Product Improvement Plan defines how to improve the **Open Applied AI Atlas** as a public knowledge base for applied AI and ML in organizations.

This plan is not a one-shot prompt and not a rigid checklist. It is a live campaign charter for large, phased improvement work. Use it to guide deep repository improvement over many passes.

Use this PIP when the work needs more than local cleanup. It is the control layer for repository-wide atlas improvement, chapter-family sequencing, milestone selection, and quality enforcement.

The atlas already has strong foundations:

* a clear mission
* a broad chapter map
* strong editorial intent
* cross-functional scope
* a durable focus on openness, sovereignty, portability, privacy, compliance, and lock-in

The remaining work is not about changing that direction. It is about turning the atlas into a stronger working guide for real organizational AI work, so readers can:

* understand what kind of system, decision, or trade-off they are dealing with
* compare real options and consequences
* plan implementation, governance, sourcing, and operating work
* carry decisions into building, review, and day-to-day operation
* trust the guidance where evidence, recommendations, and comparisons matter
* follow the writing without insider vocabulary or abstract standards-style prose

---

## 2. Product Objective

Improve the atlas so that it helps readers:

* understand the system, option, or decision surface in front of them
* decide between real choices and trade-offs
* plan implementation, governance, sourcing, and operating work
* build and run systems with clearer expectations and boundaries
* review claims, comparisons, and recommendations with more confidence
* follow the writing without needing insider language or prior atlas knowledge

The atlas should feel like a strong human guide for real organizational AI work, not like a tidy but partially scaffolded outline or a standards memo written at a distance from the reader.

### What These Quality Words Mean

In this PIP, use these words in concrete ways:

* `practical` means a page helps a reader make a real decision, plan real work, carry out an implementation step, review a system, or run part of the operating model
* `trustworthy` means recommendations, comparisons, and claims are clear about what is fact, what is atlas synthesis, and what is editorial judgment, with primary sources where needed
* `easy to read` means a page states its purpose clearly, uses direct language, explains unfamiliar terms, and avoids abstract or institutional phrasing when a plain sentence would work better
* `stronger page` means the page now has a clearer reader job, less boilerplate, more usable substance, and a better fit with its chapter than before
* `reader value` means the page helps the reader understand, choose, plan, build, govern, review, or operate something more effectively than before
* `stronger comparison` means the page compares like with like, names why the compared entities were selected, shows the control and dependence questions that change the decision, and makes the row-level trade-offs legible
* `stronger entity table` means the table covers the right entities, excludes weak filler rows, gives each row enough depth to support a decision, and makes the primary source and dependence posture visible

### How To Judge A Page

Treat a page as good only if a reader can answer questions like these quickly:

* what is this page for
* who should use it
* what decision, task, or understanding it supports
* why this page belongs here instead of being merged, split, moved, or deleted
* which terms need explanation and whether the page explains them well enough
* whether the page is trustworthy enough to reuse when it makes claims, comparisons, or recommendations
* whether its tables and comparisons are deep enough to support a real decision rather than a superficial scan

## 3. Non-Negotiable Principles

All changes must preserve and strengthen these principles:

1. **Keep the broad applied AI scope**
   Do not collapse the atlas into LLM-only content.

2. **Keep the organizational view**
   Treat AI systems as organizational systems, not only as models or vendor features.

3. **Keep the cross-functional reader model**
   The atlas must remain useful to business, product, architecture, engineering, ML, QA, security, privacy, governance, sourcing, operations, and leadership roles.

4. **Keep openness, sovereignty, portability, privacy, compliance, and lock-in visible where they matter**

5. **Favor decision utility over completeness theater**
   Pages should help the reader decide, compare, plan, implement, govern, or review.

6. **Favor stronger pages over more pages**
   Symmetry is not a product goal.

7. **Prefer primary sources and explicit evidence where trust-critical claims are made**

8. **Prefer clear, direct language over abstract expert language**

9. **Massively raise the quality bar for entity tables and comparison pages**
   Expand entity coverage where shallow selection hides the real decision, remove weak rows, compare like with like, and make row depth strong enough that a reader can use the table in actual planning, governance, sourcing, implementation, and operations work.

10. **Complete one milestone or chapter family properly before moving wider**
    Avoid broad shallow touching across the repo.

## 4. Working Doctrines

### Structural Freedom Doctrine

Nothing in the current atlas structure is sacred.

If evidence or logic shows that the atlas becomes better by changing its structure, then change the structure.

This includes:

* merging chapters or subchapters
* deleting weak or redundant pages
* splitting overloaded chapters
* reordering chapter families
* retitling sections
* renumbering chapters and subchapters
* rescoping chapter boundaries

Structural stability is not a goal by itself. Quality is the goal.

What must stay stable is not the numbering. What must stay stable is:

* mission fit
* cross-role usefulness
* taxonomy coherence
* reader usefulness
* stronger navigation after the change
* less duplication
* more honest evidence posture

### Relevance-First Doctrine

Do not add a section, pattern, artifact, or page just because it exists elsewhere.

Every addition should earn its place by materially improving reader value.

This means:

* do not add `## Evidence Notes` everywhere
* do not add tables where prose is clearer
* do not add diagrams unless they explain something better than text
* do not add worked examples unless they help the reader more than a normal explanation would
* do not add operational artifacts unless they are genuinely reusable
* do not keep a standalone page if it has no strong reason to exist

### Writing And Tone Doctrine

The atlas should speak to the reader directly.

Prefer:

* “Use this page when…”
* “If you are deciding…”
* “Ask…”
* “Check…”
* “Revisit this when…”

Avoid:

* vague institutional language
* detached standards-style prose when a practical sentence would do
* unexplained abbreviations
* jargon used instead of explanation

### Entity-Table Doctrine

When a page uses an entity table, the table must earn its place and clear a high quality bar.

This means:

* choose entities because they help the reader decide, not because they are easy to list
* increase scope when the current table hides meaningful market, tooling, standards, or architecture alternatives
* increase depth when rows are too thin to support a real decision
* split mixed entity classes unless the mixed comparison is itself the point and is explained clearly
* make entity type, purpose, control posture, openness posture, main dependence or lock-in note, and primary source visible at minimum
* add extended fields only when they change the decision
* delete weak, redundant, or low-signal rows instead of preserving symmetry
* add short framing prose so the reader knows what the table helps them decide
* add `## Evidence Notes` only when the page mixes direct facts with synthesis or judgment in a trust-critical way

### Completion Doctrine

Use milestone-first sequencing:

* complete one milestone properly before starting the next milestone
* complete one chapter family properly before moving widely into the next family
* make another pass when obvious same-pass cleanup remains
* do not stop at cosmetic rewriting if structure, evidence, or comparison quality is still weak

## 5. Core Product Problems To Solve

### Problem A: Repetition and boilerplate

Many pages still repeat framing that could appear in many chapters instead of helping the reader do something specific in this page.

### Problem B: Thin or low-value pages

Some pages still exist mainly to preserve chapter symmetry or outline shape rather than to support a distinct reader job.

### Problem C: Not enough decision-grade utility

The atlas still needs more pages that a reader can reuse directly for choice-making, planning, review, implementation, or operations work.

### Problem D: Abstract or insider language

Some pages still rely on jargon, abbreviated thinking, or policy-style language that is harder to understand than it needs to be.

### Problem E: Trust and evidence posture still uneven

Evidence Notes, sourcing clarity, and the distinction between fact, synthesis, and judgment are still too uneven across the atlas.

### Problem F: Structure may no longer match product logic

Some chapter and subchapter boundaries no longer match the actual reader jobs or topic boundaries the atlas is trying to serve.

### Problem G: Entity-table scope and depth are still too weak

Many comparison tables still choose too few entities, mix classes too casually, or give rows too little depth for real decision use.

### Problem H: Comparison quality varies too much by chapter

Some chapters compare options well while others still read like partial inventories, shallow vendor lists, or table-shaped notes.

## 6. Desired End State

After this campaign, the atlas should show the following qualities:

* readers can tell quickly what a page is for and what it helps them do
* chapter front pages are concise, useful, and easy to enter
* pages have clearer reader jobs and less overlap with neighboring pages
* weak pages are merged, rewritten, split, reordered, renamed, or removed
* the chapter map feels earned rather than inherited
* the atlas speaks directly to the reader in plain language
* pages are easier to understand without insider vocabulary or unexplained abbreviations
* recommendation-heavy and comparison-heavy pages make their evidence posture visible
* entity tables are broader where breadth matters, deeper where depth matters, and more selective where weak rows should be removed
* comparison pages explain why the compared entities belong together and what the reader should do with the result
* planning, implementation, governance, sourcing, and operations work are supported by reusable guidance, not only explanation
* the repository feels curated, confident, coherent, and readable

## 8. Milestone And Work-Stream Rules

This PIP must contain at least:

* `20` phases
* `100` milestones
* `500` work streams

The current charter uses:

* `20` phases
* `100` milestones
* `520` work streams

These counts are hard minimums, not a symmetry rule. Phase, milestone, and work-stream distribution may be uneven when uneven distribution produces a stronger, more specific, and less padded charter.

### Rules For Milestones

Each milestone must:

* represent one coherent improvement cluster
* have a reader-facing outcome
* avoid duplication with neighboring milestones
* be completable without broad shallow touching
* be describable in terms of what becomes stronger when the milestone is done

Milestone completion test:

* a milestone is complete only when its target surface is materially stronger for readers, its own work streams have landed coherently, and no obvious same-pass cleanup remains inside that surface

### Rules For Work Streams

Each work stream must:

* improve or fix one aspect derived from this PIP
* be concrete enough to implement without inventing a new brief
* help the reader more than it helps process bookkeeping
* name a structural, explanatory, comparison, evidence, or operational improvement
* identify a target surface, a weakness to address, or an outcome that can be checked afterward

### Work-Stream Shape

Use work streams for things like:

* one weak front door that needs a stronger reader job
* one comparison table family that needs broader entity selection
* one page that needs deeper row content
* one chapter boundary that needs a split or merge
* one terminology problem that needs plain-language repair
* one sourcing, evidence, or control ambiguity that needs a stronger treatment

## 10. Deliverables

The deliverables should be reader-serving atlas outcomes, not public planning scaffolding.

### Required Outcomes

1. More pages that clearly state what they help the reader decide, do, or understand.
2. Better chapter and subchapter boundaries that match actual reader jobs and topic boundaries.
3. Fewer standalone pages that exist mainly for symmetry, boilerplate, or outline padding.
4. More direct reader-facing language and fewer abstract, institutional phrases.
5. Less unnecessary jargon and fewer unexplained abbreviations.
6. Clearer evidence posture on pages that compare options, summarize claims, or make recommendations.
7. Stronger entity tables with broader and better-chosen entities where breadth matters.
8. Stronger entity tables with deeper row content where depth matters.
9. Stronger comparison logic that explains why the compared entities belong together.
10. More decision, planning, governance, and review aids where they clearly help readers do real work.
11. More worked examples where they clarify decisions or implementation paths better than explanation alone.
12. Better chapter front doors, reading paths, and navigation into the right subchapters.
13. Updated editorial or contribution guidance only if the improved atlas genuinely needs it.
14. An updated starter prompt in `prompts/PROMPT_001.md` that stays aligned with this PIP.

### Not Required By Default

Do not create public meta-docs such as:

* scoreboards
* ledgers
* parity matrices
* public audit inventories
* controller-serving planning artifacts

unless they are truly reader-facing and belong in the atlas.

## 11. Success Metrics

### Reader Experience Signals

A new reader should be able to say:

* “I understand what this atlas is for.”
* “I know what this page is helping me decide or do.”
* “This speaks clearly to me.”
* “I do not need insider vocabulary to follow the argument.”
* “I can carry this guidance into planning, implementation, governance, sourcing, or operations work.”
* “The pages do not all sound the same.”
* “I can tell which claims are facts, which are synthesis, and which are judgment.”
* “Different roles on my team can work from the same understanding.”
* “These comparison tables are actually helping me choose or review something.”

### Quality Signals

* less repeated framing that could fit almost any chapter
* more pages with a clear reader job
* clearer chapter and subchapter differentiation
* clearer direct-reader tone
* plainer language
* fewer weak standalone pages
* clearer evidence posture on recommendation-heavy and comparison-heavy pages
* better support for decisions, planning, implementation, governance, sourcing, review, and operation
* broader and better-chosen entities in touched comparison tables
* deeper and more decision-useful rows in touched comparison tables
* fewer mixed-class tables that hide the real comparison problem

### Structural Signals

* chapter reshaping produces clearer boundaries and easier navigation instead of merely moving text around
* renumbering or reordering produces a chapter sequence that is easier to read and extend
* more pages clearly justify why they exist and where they belong
* touched chapter families feel stronger as families, not only as individual pages

## 12. Anti-Patterns

Reject the following:

1. preserving weak structure because renumbering feels expensive
2. adding the same section everywhere
3. broad low-signal touching across many chapters
4. abstract, standards-like prose where direct language is possible
5. jargon used instead of explanation
6. unexplained abbreviations
7. keeping pages alive because they already exist
8. treating stability as a higher value than quality
9. filling the repo with public planning scaffolding
10. confusing polish with usefulness
11. mistaking a shallow entity table for a real comparison
12. keeping weak rows for symmetry, familiarity, or count inflation
13. mixing vendors, tools, standards, and architectures without naming why
14. using vendor claims as atlas voice
15. updating the PIP without updating the starter prompt that points at it

## 13. Done When

Work is done only when it materially improves the repo or atlas, preserves mission and scope, avoids duplication, keeps numbering and links coherent, updates affected guidance and prompts, removes temporary scaffolding, deepens comparison and entity-table quality where relevant, and leaves no obvious same-pass cleanup.

Use this PIP as a live charter for bold, careful, evidence-aware atlas improvement.
