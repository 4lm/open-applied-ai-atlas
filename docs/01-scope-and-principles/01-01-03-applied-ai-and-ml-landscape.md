# 1.1.3 Applied AI And ML Landscape

The atlas is deliberately broader than LLM applications. Organizational AI and ML includes multiple technical families with different governance, evaluation, sourcing, and operational implications.

This file matters because the chapter on scope and principles depends on readers being able to move from broad chapter framing to a sharper local distinction, journey, or scope rule. The atlas works only when even small files carry enough narrative framing to tell the reader why the material exists and what it should change in practice.

## Why This File Matters

The point here is not length for its own sake. The point is to make the surrounding chapter more usable by adding explanation, selective bullets, and a more explicit reading posture around the existing material.

## Covered System Families

- Conversational and assistive systems: chat systems, copilots, coding assistants, coding agents, workflow agents.
- Knowledge-heavy systems: document intelligence, retrieval systems, memory systems.
- Predictive and decision-support systems: forecasting, recommender systems, anomaly detection, optimization systems.
- Perception systems: computer vision and speech systems.
- Model method families: classical ML, deep learning, generative AI, and hybrid systems.
- Adaptation and capability layers: fine-tuning and training systems.

## Why The Broader Landscape Matters

The same organization may run a recommendation engine, a forecasting workflow, a document extraction system, and an agentic assistant at the same time. Those systems share some infrastructure and governance concerns, but they do not fail in the same ways and should not be governed as if they were identical.

## Common Failure Mode

A repository that implicitly narrows itself to current GenAI tooling loses usefulness for architecture, procurement, privacy, and assurance readers who need a broader operational picture. This chapter exists to prevent that collapse.
