# 16.2.1 Worked Oversight Scenarios

This subsection turns the chapter into concrete organizational situations so the abstractions can be checked against real delivery contexts.

These examples turn the chapter into concrete organizational situations. They are deliberately short and comparative so the reader can see where the chapter changes a real decision.

## Central platform with domain services

A company centralizes shared controls but leaves service accountability with domain teams.

- What this example makes visible: covers the human roles, approval patterns, organizational operating models, and decision rights required to run applied AI systems responsibly and at scale.
- What to watch for: The failure mode is letting the platform team absorb ownership by default.

## Early federated pilots

Domain teams move quickly but share taxonomy and review forums to prevent drift.

- What this example makes visible: covers the human roles, approval patterns, organizational operating models, and decision rights required to run applied AI systems responsibly and at scale.
- What to watch for: The failure mode is treating pilot freedom as a reason to skip governance.

## High-risk advisory workflow

An advisory system gets explicit sign-off rights because the business consequence is high even without direct automation.

- What this example makes visible: covers the human roles, approval patterns, organizational operating models, and decision rights required to run applied AI systems responsibly and at scale.
- What to watch for: The failure mode is underestimating advisory risk.

## Portfolio-scale change control

A shared operating rhythm is created once model, routing, and policy changes affect many services.

- What this example makes visible: covers the human roles, approval patterns, organizational operating models, and decision rights required to run applied AI systems responsibly and at scale.
- What to watch for: The failure mode is keeping all review mechanisms informal as scale grows.
