# 16. Human Oversight And Operating Model

_Page Type: Chapter Index | Maturity: Draft_

This chapter turns "human oversight" into named authority, staffed intervention, and reviewable evidence. Use it when the real question is not whether a person appears somewhere in the workflow, but whether someone can actually approve, stop, override, or escalate the system once consequences become real.

Oversight fails when ownership, approval rights, escalation lanes, or vendor-mediated controls stay implicit until an incident, audit, or regulator asks who was really in charge.

## How To Read This Chapter

- Start with [16.1 Oversight Foundations](16-01-00-oversight-foundations.md) when the team still needs a clean role map, explicit decision rights, and a usable lane for notification, approval, expert review, or stop-and-redesign governance.
- Move to [16.2 Operating The Human Model](16-02-00-operating-the-human-model.md) when the oversight posture is plausible on paper and now has to survive rollout pressure, queue design, exception handling, reviewer capacity, and intervention drills.
- Use [16.3 Reference Points](16-03-00-reference-points.md) after the operating model is clear enough that standards, control packs, and other named references will sharpen the review instead of replacing it.

## What This Chapter Helps Decide

- which actions stay advisory, which require human approval, and which should remain forbidden until the operating model is redesigned
- who owns outcome risk, who may intervene or stop the service, and where platform teams or vendors assist without becoming the hidden decision-maker
- what approval packets, escalation paths, exception records, and portability notes must exist before broader rollout
- when the unresolved problem has shifted into governance, gateway enforcement, observability, security, sourcing, or supplier-dependence review rather than oversight framing alone
