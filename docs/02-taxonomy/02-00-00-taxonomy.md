# 2. Taxonomy

_Page Type: Chapter Index | Maturity: Review-Ready_

This chapter is the atlas control surface for classifying what kind of thing a team is actually discussing before it compares options, assigns controls, narrows suppliers, or designs operating reviews. It keeps tools, vendors, models, standards, controls, architectures, and use cases from collapsing into one mixed category and gives later chapters a shared comparison language.

Use it when a proposal, shortlist, architecture note, policy draft, or standards discussion sounds plausible but still feels unstable. It is for product, architecture, engineering, governance, privacy, security, sourcing, operations, and leadership readers who need one shared language before they move into stack, implementation, review, or market decisions.

Taxonomy is a prerequisite for a trustworthy comparison table. Skipping this layer usually gives teams a stronger-looking table than they deserve, sends governance and privacy review into the wrong lane, and attaches sourcing or operating decisions to the wrong layer of the system.

The same discipline applies to chat assistants, forecasting systems, recommender pipelines, computer-vision workflows, classical ML platforms, and hybrid stacks. Taxonomy is not an LLM-only cleanup step.

## Use This Chapter When

- a page, meeting, or shortlist is mixing products, vendors, models, standards, controls, or architectures as if they were one class of decision
- a comparison table feels unstable and you need to fix the categories before widening, deepening, ranking, or trusting it
- a local chapter term needs to be normalized back to reusable atlas language
- the team needs to decide which later chapter owns the real decision

## What This Chapter Helps Decide

- what kind of entity is actually being discussed or compared
- which dimensions belong in a reusable comparison and which are distracting
- whether a comparison table should be split, deepened, widened, or removed instead of preserved in weak form
- when a local term should be normalized back to atlas language
- whether the real next step belongs in governance review, sourcing analysis, or operating-model design rather than in one more mixed table
- which downstream chapter should own the next decision once the categories are stable
